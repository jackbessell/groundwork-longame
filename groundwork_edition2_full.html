The thirty year question nobody asks at the start
What does patient capital actually need to see before it backs a nature project?

We had known since 2019 that deer fencing would be necessary. The evidence was written into the land. Stand anywhere in the ancient woodland and you could read it: every branch browsed to bare wood at the height a deer can reach, blaeberry held to barely a centimetre off the ground, the wildflower layer largely absent. The baseline surveys confirmed what the eye already knew. Deer numbers in the area were well above the threshold at which natural regeneration becomes viable, and the ancient woodland was not reproducing. So my father and I sat down with a pen and paper to work out what to do about it. Every first step revealed ten things that needed to be in place before it could begin, and each of those pulled a decade of maintenance behind it. What started as a project question became a mind map of interconnected decisions, and the mind map grew sideways before it grew forward.

This is the moment most nature finance conversations are not prepared for. Not because the landholder doesn't know their land, and not because the investor doesn't understand nature projects in principle, but because neither has yet looked at the whole thing together: the ecological outcomes, the implementation approach, the maintenance logic, the local constraints. Add to that the personal philosophy of the landholder who will actually deliver it, and the commercial reality of doing all of that across a timeframe that may outlast the landholder in the room. Projects do get financed and delivered when this thinking is done early and done properly. The question is who helps a landholder get there.

The gap between available capital and invested projects is not primarily about capital availability. Patient capital exists. Impact investors are looking. What remains stubbornly wide is something more structural. Most landholders think in annual cycles, five years at most, cash flowing in and out in a rhythm that matches how land has always been managed. What nature finance asks for is fundamentally different. Investment arrives upfront. The obligation runs for thirty years. And maintenance, which in a conventional land management conversation happens at the end of the planning process, has to move to the beginning. Because the cost of maintaining what you create is not separable from the cost of creating it, and any investor who understands this will want to see that you understand it too. The investors who tend to grasp this most readily are those who have financed other long duration infrastructure, roads, utilities, regulated assets, where the discipline of modelling obligations across decades is already second nature.

What this means in practice is that an investment case for a nature project is not primarily a document about what you will do. It is a document about what it will take to keep doing it. Who will repair the fencing in year eight. How you will source materials and labour if you are remote and the contractor who built the infrastructure no longer operates in your area. What happens to your grazing approach if you are managing for regeneration and you do not own cattle, and whether the local farmer you plan to rent from will still be farming on the same terms in fifteen years. How you have factored inflation into a maintenance budget built on today's costs. What your contingency is when something fails, and something will fail, and the plan is stronger when it has looked at that before capital commits.

None of this is meant to be overwhelming, though it often is at first. The constraint of a thirty year obligation forces the kind of thinking that reveals economies of scope you would not otherwise have found. Our own experience of the fencing question illustrates this as well as anything. The upfront capital cost is significant, and it sits early in almost every natural regeneration project. But when you map what the fencing requires across time, repair schedules, stalking arrangements, the philosophy of how the cull is managed and where the venison goes, you start to see that these are not just cost lines. They are decisions about how the project connects to the local economy, to local labour, to local food systems. The constraint produces the question. The question, asked with genuine curiosity about what you know and what others in the room know and what only the combination of both can reveal, produces something more coherent than either would have reached alone. What started as an overwhelming mind map distilled, over time, into something relatively clear.

What I don't know yet is how investors who want a replicable model will handle the fact that maintenance logic is deeply site specific. The variables that shape a thirty year operating model, remoteness, local labour markets, personal landholder capacity, seasonal access, personal philosophy, are not standardised across projects. The tension between an investor's need for replicable models and the site-specific reality of maintenance logic hasn't been resolved yet. That's not a failure of intent on either side. It's a structural gap the market is still working out how to bridge. And smaller or newer landholders coming into this space for the first time, without lived experience of what adaptive management actually requires across decades, will not know what they don't know until the plan is already committed.

The question I keep returning to is this: who is responsible for helping a landholder see the whole picture before they sign, across the ecological, commercial, legal, and operational dimensions that a thirty year commitment actually requires? That role demands someone who can hold all of those threads simultaneously, who understands where the ecology and the finance and the logistics and the personal philosophy connect, and who has enough experience across domains to know what the landholder does not yet know they need to ask. The capability exists in places. The question is whether it reaches enough landholders early enough, and whether the market creates the conditions for it to do so at the scale the opportunity actually requires.

These are my own views, formed through direct experience in Scotland's land and nature finance landscape since 2019. Unless stated they do not represent the positions of any organisations I'm associated with.

This piece focuses specifically on the Scottish context and does not describe the funding landscape in England or elsewhere.
